# Symmetry-Breaking Joint-Permutation Optimization: Benchmark Report

## Overview

**Optimization**: Instead of enumerating 2744 ordered (s0,s1,s2) triples and checking each independently,
enumerate the 560 distinct **unordered** triples and check all their permutations jointly in a single
(ex, pos) pass. Each bit-check contributes to all permutations' truth tables simultaneously.

**Expected improvement**: ~2.4× fewer bit-checks for arity-3 wrong candidates.
At budget 3500: covers 72.9% of unordered search space vs 36.4% ordered.

**Full dataset**: 1602 bit-manipulation problems.

## Analysis: Why the Optimization Backfired

**Theory predicted 2.4× improvement; practice shows 1.7× regression for arity-3.**

### What the theory said

- 2744 ordered triples → 560 unordered groups (avg 4.9 permutations each)
- Each wrong ordered triple costs E[T] ≈ 3.5 bit-checks (birthday paradox, K=8 slots)
- Joint check of n_p=6 permutations costs E[max(T₁…T₆)] ≈ H₆ × 3.5 ≈ 8.6 bit-checks
- Net: 2744×3.5 = 9604 vs 560×8.6 = 4816 — theoretically 2× fewer bit-checks

### Why it doesn't hold in practice

**The all-permutations-must-fail condition forces the group to pay for its slowest member.**

In infer3, each ordered triple is abandoned the moment a single contradiction is found. Fast failure propagates immediately to the next candidate. The average cost per wrong triple is ~3.5 bit-checks because early contradictions terminate early.

In infer5, the (ex, pos) loop for an unordered group cannot terminate until every permutation has found a contradiction. If 5 of 6 permutations fail after ~3 bit-checks but the 6th takes ~25, the entire group costs ~25 bit-checks — far above the theoretical 8.6.

**VSIDS hot_pos is degraded.** In infer3, hot_pos is updated once per ordered triple failure, stabilizing to the most discriminating position across many sequential tests. In infer5, hot_pos is updated multiple times within a single group (once per failing permutation), leaving it set to the failure position of the *last* permutation — which may be the least discriminating position for the next group.

**Consequence for key metric:**
- At 5000 bit-check budget: infer3 = 77.8% accuracy; infer5 = 71.0% accuracy (-108 problems)
- The optimization made things substantially worse at every budget ≥ 2000.

### Conclusion

The symmetry-breaking joint-permutation approach is a negative result. The infer3 solver (frequency-ordered s-values + hot_pos VSIDS, no symmetry breaking) remains the better arity-3 strategy. The theoretical argument failed because the birthday-problem analysis treats permutation failures as independent and bounded by their mean; in practice the tailing permutation dominates and disrupts hot_pos for subsequent groups.
